Alta’s Story

A mother of three, a teacher by training, and a conservationist by heart, I have spent my career at the intersection of nomadic wisdom and modern science. After completing my Master’s in Environmental Science at Melbourne University, I felt a deep pull to return to these landscapes. I realized that the “Mongolian Calmness”—the stillness that protects our nomadic culture and our wildlife—is a treasure that I needed to help preserve.

Our June Journey: Seven Days in the Altai

Our journey begins the moment you land at Khovd airport. From there, we leave the town behind and head toward the high Altai. We do not follow a strict hourly itinerary; we let the weather, the light, and the movement of the animals guide our path through the province.

  • Into the High Altai: We base ourselves in a mountain ger camp situated in the rugged heights. This is the silent home of the Snow Leopard. We move through this terrain as observers, hoping for a glimpse of these “ghosts,” while keeping our eyes on the ridges for Ibex and Argali sheep.
  • The Shore of a Prehistoric Sea: We travel to Durgun Lake, a massive saltwater basin that remains from ancient times. Here, the sand dunes meet the deep water, creating a landscape of stark contrasts and profound quiet.
  • Ancient Galleries: We trek to the Three Sengher Caves (Gurvan Senheriin Agui), Ishgen Tolgoi, and Nuhen Otgiin Buuts. These sites are our ancestors’ libraries, filled with rock paintings and carvings dating back 20,000 years.
  • The Steppe & The Sky: We cross the vast plains of Khomiin Tal National Park, the sanctuary of the Takhi (Przewalski’s wild horse). As we drive and hike, we look for Gazelles darting across the horizon and Marmots near the rocky outcrops.
  • The Waters of Khar Us Nuur: Our path takes us by the “Black Water Lake,” where the vast reed beds are alive with movement.
